# Ogilvy Headline & Copy Techniques

> Extracted from David Ogilvy's framework library. Technique reference only — no voice mimicry.

---

## The Research-First Discipline

Before writing a single word:
- Use the product personally. Know how it works, feels, performs.
- Read every competitor ad for the last 20 years. The opportunity lives in the unsaid.
- Research the consumer: what language do they use? What are their objections?
- Study direct response results — tested principles beat gut instinct.

---

## The Big Idea Test

A Big Idea must pass ALL five checks:
1. Did it make me gasp when I first saw it?
2. Do I wish I had thought of it?
3. Is it unique (not derivative)?
4. Does it fit the strategy perfectly?
5. Could it run for 30 years without getting tired?

**Swap test:** If you can replace your brand name with a competitor's and it still works, you don't have a Big Idea.

---

## 9 Headline Principles

1. **Include the brand name.** 80% of readers only see the headline.
2. **Include the selling promise.** Every headline tells the reader what's in it for them.
3. **Inject news.** Words that trigger attention: "new," "introducing," "announcing," "now," "finally."
4. **Be specific, not clever.** "Profit of 5%" beats "low profit margin." Specificity = credibility.
5. **Use numbers.** Specific > round. Odd numbers feel more authentic than even.
6. **Never use puns or double meanings.** Nobody stops to decode cleverness.
7. **Never use negatives.** Readers skip "NOT" — frame everything positively.
8. **Flag your audience.** If you're writing for PMMs, put "PMM" in the headline.
9. **End with a lure into the body.** Create enough curiosity to pull them forward.

**Length:** 6-12 words is the sweet spot. But a 17-word headline that says something beats a 5-word headline that says nothing.

**Process:** Write 16+ headlines before choosing. Your first idea is rarely your best.

---

## Body Copy Rules

- **Write like you talk.** No jargon. Pretend you're explaining to a smart person at a party.
- **Lead with the most important fact.** Lose them in paragraph one and nothing else matters.
- **Be specific at every turn.** "37 out of 40 test users reported improvement within 14 days" > "Our product works."
- **Use subheadings every few paragraphs.** Most readers skim before they read.
- **Long copy outsells short** for considered purchases. Between 50 and 500 words, drop-off is marginal.
- **Editorial format outperforms ad format.** Content that feels like information beats content that feels like selling.

---

## The P.S. Rule

The P.S. is the second most-read element after the headline. Use it for your strongest guarantee or most compelling benefit.
