# Newsletter Article Practices — PMM Brain-Native

---

## Newsletter = The Pillar

The newsletter article is the deepest content format. It's the pillar from which LinkedIn posts, Twitter threads, and carousels are atomized. Write the newsletter FIRST when generating for all formats.

---

## Structure

### 1. Subject Line (Ogilvy Rules Applied)

Write 5 variations using these patterns:
- **Curiosity + specificity:** "The $47 mistake that cost us 3 months of pipeline"
- **Contrarian:** "Stop writing competitive battlecards (do this instead)"
- **Story tease:** "A lost deal taught me more about positioning than any framework"
- **Stat drop:** "94% of B2B brands sound the same. Here's the math on why."
- **Personal:** "I almost killed our win-loss program last quarter"

**Subject line rules:**
- Include the promise — what will they learn?
- Be specific, not clever. Numbers > adjectives.
- 6-12 words. Long enough to include the promise, short enough to scan.
- Never clickbait. The article must deliver on the subject line.
- Load expert technique: `experts/ogilvy-headlines.md` for headline principles.

### 2. Preview Text

Extends the subject line's promise. Adds new information that increases open motivation. Never repeats the subject line.

### 3. Opening — Story or Hook (200-400 words)

Start with ONE of:
- **A specific story** — A conversation, a moment, a failure, a discovery. Time, place, action. No preamble.
- **A jarring stat** — A number that breaks expectations. Then immediately contextualize it.
- **A scene** — Drop the reader into a situation. Dialogue, stakes, tension.

**Never start with:** "In today's newsletter..." / "Welcome back to..." / "I've been thinking about..."

The opening must earn the reader's attention in the first 2 sentences. Everything else is negotiable.

### 4. Bridge to Insight (100-200 words)

Connect the story/hook to the core insight. The bridge should feel natural, not forced.
- "And that's exactly what happens when..."
- "This is the same pattern I see in..."
- "The reason this matters for PMMs is..."

### 5. Deep Framework / Evidence Section (600-1200 words)

This is the meat. The section that justifies the newsletter format over a LinkedIn post.

**Requirements:**
- **Minimum 3 evidence anchors** from `evidence_bank` — real stats, case studies, cited research
- **At least one framework or mental model** from `synthesized_mental_models`
- **Cross-reference at least 2 newsletter sources** — shows breadth of reading
- **Include one "only-I-could-have-written-this" section** (200+ words) — operator experience, specific teardown, personal case study, or original framework combination

**Structure options:**
- **Progressive build:** Premise → Layer 1 → Layer 2 → Layer 3 → Synthesis
- **Framework breakdown:** Context → Steps/components → Application → Common mistakes
- **Comparative analysis:** Approach A vs B → Evidence for each → Synthesis → Recommendation
- **Teardown:** Pick a real example → Walk through what works → What doesn't → Lessons

**Depth rules:**
- Include counterarguments. Address the "but what about..." before the reader thinks it.
- Include practitioner wisdom from `topic_depth_layers` — key debates, common mistakes.
- Use rejection-based teaching: show the wrong way before the right way.
- Use the "kinda like" bridge (from `experts/brunson-storytelling.md`) for complex concepts.

### 6. Practitioner Callout — "What Most Get Wrong" (200-400 words)

A dedicated section that signals operator credibility:
- Name the 2-3 most common mistakes practitioners make on this topic
- Explain WHY they make them (not just that they're wrong)
- Show the better approach with specific examples

This section is what separates a newsletter from a blog post. It requires having DONE the thing.

### 7. Landing (100-200 words)

- Restate the core insight in one powerful paragraph
- Connect back to the opening story/stat if possible (creates narrative closure)
- End with a specific, actionable takeaway — not a generic "food for thought"

### 8. P.S. (1-3 sentences)

Second most-read element after the subject line. Use for:
- A secondary CTA
- A personal note or behind-the-scenes detail
- A teaser for the next issue
- A repeat of the main CTA in different words

### 9. Single CTA

One call to action per newsletter. Don't fragment attention.
- Reply to this email
- Share with a colleague
- Check out [specific resource]
- Try [specific exercise] this week

## Word Count Targets

| Section | Target |
|---------|--------|
| Subject line | 6-12 words |
| Opening | 200-400 words |
| Bridge | 100-200 words |
| Deep section | 600-1200 words |
| Practitioner callout | 200-400 words |
| Landing | 100-200 words |
| P.S. | 1-3 sentences |
| **Total** | **1500-3000 words** |

---

## Evidence Density

Newsletters demand the highest evidence density of any format:
- **Minimum 3 evidence anchors** (stats, case studies, research citations)
- **Minimum 2 newsletter source cross-references**
- **Minimum 1 mental model application**
- Prefer: Wynter surveys, Kyle Poyar benchmarks, PMA research, Kramer frameworks

---

## Voice & AI Decontamination

Same rules as LinkedIn (see `generation-angles.md`), with these additions:
- Longer form means MORE risk of AI patterns creeping in. Be extra vigilant.
- Vary paragraph length more aggressively. Mix 1-sentence paragraphs with 3-sentence ones.
- Use section headers that are specific, not generic. "Why your battlecard fails on slide 3" > "Common Mistakes"
- The Seinfeld Email philosophy: entertaining first, educational second, promotional third.
