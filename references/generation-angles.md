# 3 Angles Model — Generation Instructions

Every theme produces 3 posts, each with a different angle and engagement goal.

---

## Hook Typology — Use One of These Four Patterns

Every post MUST open with one of these hook types. No warm-up. No throat-clearing. Line 1 earns line 2.

### Type 1: STAT DROP
Lead with a specific, jarring number. The number does the work.
- "94% of B2B SaaS companies admit their brands lack differentiation."
- "Cognism saw a 40% uptick in conversions after one homepage change."
- "68% of B2B companies don't need more budget. They need permission to take creative risks."

### Type 2: SCENE DROP
Drop the reader into a moment — dialogue, action, or a specific situation. No setup.
- "PMM: 'My message is clear, I WIN!' Buyer, visiting site: 'OK, got it.' Two weeks later: 'What was that product again?'"
- "The VP of Sales slid a battlecard across the table. 'This is useless.'"

### Type 3: NAME + CLAIM
Name a real company or person, then make a bold observation about them.
- "Positioning an SMB/prosumer product for the enterprise is hard. Canva is giving us a masterclass."
- "Gong's homepage does something most Series C companies are afraid to do."

### Type 4: BEFORE/AFTER
Show a specific contrast. The gap IS the hook.
- "From 'The world's best global sales intelligence platform' to 'We give you mobile phone numbers and B2B emails of people you want to do business with.'"
- "'We help teams collaborate better' → 'Designers ship 2x faster because reviewers comment on the design, not a screenshot of it.'"

### Type 5: HOW-TO CLAIM
State what you'll teach and why the common approach fails. The "how to" earns the click, the rejection earns trust.
- "How to run a win/loss program that sales actually reads (not the 40-page PDF nobody opens)."
- "How to position against a market leader without mentioning them."
- "How to write a homepage headline that passes the 5-second test."

Best for: Angle 2 (Framework) posts. Use sparingly — max 1 in 3 posts. Overuse turns your feed into a tutorial channel.

### Hook Anti-Patterns — NEVER do these:
- Questions as hooks ("Ever wondered why...?", "What if I told you...?")
- "Here's the thing about X" — vague, earns nothing
- Abstract statements ("Positioning is the foundation of PMM")
- Throat-clearing ("I've been thinking a lot about X lately")
- Self-referential ("I posted about X last week and...")

## Angle 1: OPINIONATED REFRAME

**Goal:** Highest comment potential. People add nuance or share their own experience.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook**: Use hook typology above. Must be concrete from word 1.
2. **Setup**: 1-2 sentences max. What's the common approach and why it feels logical.
3. **Turn**: The evidence/experience that breaks the common view. Be specific.
4. **Build**: Why this reframe is more useful. Use evidence anchors. Keep it tight — no padding.
5. **Land**: One punchy line. Period.

**Calibration — Opinionated, NOT super contrarian:**
- YES: "Most PMMs over-invest in competitive battlecards and under-invest in the one thing that actually wins deals: the demo narrative." (opinionated, specific, backed by experience)
- NO: "Competitive intel is a waste of time." (contrarian for shock value, easily dismissed)
- YES: "Your messaging doc isn't failing because of the words. It's failing because three teams wrote it." (earned observation, makes reader reflect)
- NO: "Messaging documents are useless." (hot take with no nuance)

**The test:** Would a thoughtful PMM leader disagree with some part of this? Good — that creates conversation. Would they roll their eyes? Bad — that's hot-take LinkedIn.

**Rules:**
- Must be a genuine opinion earned from experience, not a strawman or hot take
- Include at least 1 evidence anchor
- 800-1200 characters (tight, not padded)
- No hashtags, no "Agree?", no generic CTAs

---

## Angle 2: FRAMEWORK / MENTAL MODEL

**Goal:** Highest share/save potential. Reader gets a reusable tool.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook**: Use hook typology above. Name the problem the framework solves, or drop a stat that shows why it matters.
2. **Context**: 1-2 sentences. The situation that revealed this framework.
3. **Framework**: Present clearly — numbered steps, 2x2, or simple structure. No filler between items.
4. **Application**: Show how it works with ONE specific example. Not hypothetical.
5. **Takeaway**: One line on when to use it.

**Format options:**
- **Paragraph-light:** Numbered steps where each step = 1 line instruction + 1 line rationale (no filler paragraphs between). Best for actionable frameworks.
- **Prose:** Short paragraphs building the framework. Best for conceptual mental models (2x2s, spectrums).
- Choose based on the framework. If it's steps → go paragraph-light. If it's a lens/model → go prose.

**Rules:**
- Framework must come from EXPERIENCE, not theory
- Include at least 1 evidence anchor
- 800-1200 characters
- Use white space, numbered lists, or clear structure

---

## Angle 3: STORY-LED WITH EARNED INSIGHT

**Goal:** Highest reaction potential. Reader FEELS something.

**Structure:**
1. **Hook**: Use hook typology above. Drop reader into a specific moment. Time, place, action. No preamble.
2. **Story**: What happened. Specific — names (or anonymized roles), decisions, stakes. Keep it moving.
3. **Observation**: What you noticed that others missed. The small detail.
4. **Insight**: The non-obvious lesson. NOT generic. Requires having been there.
5. **Landing**: One sentence. Reframes the story into a broader principle.

**Rules:**
- Story must feel SPECIFIC and REAL
- Include at least 1 evidence anchor
- 800-1200 characters
- Insight must be EARNED through the story, not bolted on
- Avoid "I learned that..." — show, don't tell

## Writing Mechanics

### Brevity
- Every sentence must earn its place. If removing a sentence doesn't weaken the argument, remove it.
- One idea per paragraph. One paragraph = 1-2 sentences max.
- No transition sentences ("Let me explain why", "Here's what I mean", "Think about it this way"). Just say the thing.
- No warm-up paragraphs. Start at the point, not before it.
- Prefer short sentences. Break long sentences into two.
- Target 800-1200 characters, not 1500. Shorter is almost always better.

### Rejection-Based Teaching
Show what's wrong BEFORE (or alongside) what's right. This is how operators talk — they've seen the failure mode, so they lead with it.

**Pattern:** [Right way] + "Not [wrong way]. That's how you get [bad outcome]."

PMM examples:
- "Write for one buyer persona per page. Not three. That's how you get a homepage that converts nobody."
- "Test the headline with 5 prospects before the messaging doc. Not after. That's how you end up rewriting everything in week 6."
- "'We help teams collaborate' → 'Designers ship 2x faster because reviewers comment on the design, not a screenshot of it.' One is a category. The other is a reason to buy."

**Rules for rejection-based teaching:**
- The rejection must be SPECIFIC — not "don't do bad things"
- Name the actual bad outcome, not a vague one
- Keep it to 1-2 rejections per post. More than that becomes a rant.
- Works best in Angle 1 (Reframe) and Angle 2 (Framework) posts

### Sentence Style
- Fragments are fine. "Like that." "Period." "Not even close."
- Use → arrows for before/after or cause/effect instead of wordy transitions
- Repeat the right word rather than cycling synonyms. If it's "positioning", say "positioning" five times.
- Second person ("you") over third person ("PMMs should") — direct address holds attention
- Zero hedging in the main claim. You can acknowledge nuance in the build, not in the hook or landing.

## AI Decontamination — Hard Constraints

These patterns are AI tells. They MUST NOT appear in any draft. Violating these is a critique failure.

### Word-Level Bans
- **Filler phrases:** "In order to" (use "to"), "Due to the fact that" (use "because"), "It is worth noting that" (delete), "It's important to remember" (delete)
- **AI vocabulary:** "Additionally", "Furthermore", "Moreover", "testament", "landscape" (as metaphor), "showcasing", "delve", "realm", "crucial", "pivotal", "robust", "comprehensive", "streamline", "harness"
- **Copula avoidance:** Never write "serves as", "functions as", "acts as" when you mean "is". Never write "features" or "boasts" when you mean "has".
- **Promotional adjectives:** "groundbreaking", "breathtaking", "cutting-edge", "innovative", "transformative", "revolutionary"

### Sentence-Level Bans
- **Negative parallelisms:** "It's not just X, it's Y" — just state Y directly
- **Synonym cycling:** Don't call the same thing by 4 different names. Pick the best word and repeat it.
- **Rule of three:** Don't force three-item lists ("innovation, inspiration, and insights"). Use the real number of items.
- **Hedging stacks:** "could potentially possibly" — one qualifier max. Prefer "may" or commit to the claim.
- **Significance inflation:** Don't call things "pivotal" or "a defining moment." Say what happened and let the reader judge.
- **False ranges:** "from X to Y" used to sound comprehensive. Just list the real things.

### Structure-Level Bans
- **Balanced hedging:** Don't present both sides equally and conclude with "it depends." Take a position.
- **Generic conclusions:** "The future looks bright" / "Time will tell" / "Only time will tell." End with something specific or don't end at all.
- **Setup-payoff padding:** No "Let's explore why" → [3 paragraphs] → "As we've seen." Cut the bookends.
- **Sycophantic framing:** No "Great question!" or "This is such an important topic." Just answer.

### The Sniff Test
Read the draft aloud. If any sentence sounds like it could appear in a ChatGPT response to any prompt on any topic, rewrite it. Every sentence should sound like a specific person talking about a specific thing.

## Shared Rules (All Angles)

- Write in the user's voice, not generic LinkedIn voice
- Every post MUST have at least 1 evidence anchor (stat, case study, analogy, cited study)
- Every post must pass the "only THIS person could have written this" test
- Use white space and short paragraphs (1-2 sentences max per paragraph)
- No hashtags, no "Agree? 👇", no "Thoughts?", no generic CTAs
- Forbidden: "In today's fast-paced world", "It's no secret", "Let's dive in", "Game-changer", "Unpack", "leverage"

## Format Adaptations

The 3 angles and 5 hook types work across ALL formats. Here's how each adapts.

### Newsletter Angle Structures

**Angle 1 (Opinionated Reframe) → Newsletter:**
- Opening: Scene or story that reveals why the common view feels logical (200-400 words)
- Bridge: "But here's what the data actually shows..." (100-200 words)
- Deep section: Full evidence build with 3+ anchors, counterarguments addressed, practitioner callout (600-1200 words)
- Landing: The reframe, sharper than the LinkedIn version because you've earned it through depth

**Angle 2 (Framework) → Newsletter:**
- Opening: The moment that revealed the framework — full Epiphany Bridge (7-step from `experts/brunson-storytelling.md`)
- Deep section: Complete framework walkthrough — context per step, examples per step, common mistakes per step
- Practitioner callout: "Where most teams get stuck implementing this"
- Landing: One-line summary of when to use the framework

**Angle 3 (Story-Led) → Newsletter:**
- Opening: Full narrative — time, place, characters, stakes, dialogue (400+ words)
- Bridge: The small detail that revealed the insight
- Deep section: Why this matters beyond the story — evidence, models, broader pattern
- Landing: Circle back to the opening scene

### Twitter/X Angle Structures

**Angle 1 (Opinionated Reframe) → Hot Take Thread:**
- Hook tweet: Bold claim (280 chars)
- Tweets 2-3: "Here's why" + evidence
- Tweets 4-5: Examples or counter-examples
- Tweet 6: Address the obvious objection
- Tweet 7: The reframe
- Close: CTA

**Angle 2 (Framework) → Framework Breakdown Thread:**
- Hook tweet: Framework name + result + "Most [audience] miss this:"
- Tweet 2: Why the old way doesn't work
- Tweets 3-8: One step per tweet
- Tweet 9: Common mistakes
- Close: Summary + CTA

**Angle 3 (Story-Led) → Story Thread:**
- Hook tweet: Drop into the moment + stakes
- Tweets 2-4: Context and rising tension
- Tweets 5-6: The turning point
- Tweets 7-8: Result and aftermath
- Tweet 9: The lesson
- Close: CTA

### Carousel Angle Structures

**Angle 1 (Opinionated Reframe) → Myth-Busting Carousel:**
- Cover: "X Things Most PMMs Get Wrong About [Topic]"
- Slides 2-7: Myth → Reality per slide
- Slide 8: The core reframe
- Close: CTA + brand

**Angle 2 (Framework) → Framework Walkthrough Carousel:**
- Cover: Framework name + outcome
- Slide 2: Context / why this matters
- Slides 3-7: One step per slide
- Slide 8: Application example
- Close: CTA + brand

**Angle 3 (Story-Led) → Teardown Carousel:**
- Cover: "How [Company/Person] [Did Something]"
- Slides 2-4: Setup + what they did
- Slides 5-7: Analysis of each key decision
- Slide 8: Lessons
- Close: CTA + brand
